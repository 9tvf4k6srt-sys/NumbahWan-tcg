📊 The Wyckoff Price Cycle

Richard D. Wyckoff (1873-1934) developed a method to understand market movements by analyzing the actions of large institutional operators, which he called the "Composite Man". The market moves in recurring cycles consisting of four distinct phases.

Accumulation Phase

  • Large institutions quietly buy shares at low prices
  • Occurs after a prolonged downtrend (markdown)
  • Price moves sideways within a trading range
  • Supply is gradually absorbed by demand
  • Ends with a "Spring" or successful test of support

Markup Phase

  • Price breaks out above the trading range
  • Demand exceeds supply consistently
  • Higher highs and higher lows form an uptrend
  • Public interest and volume increase
  • Re-accumulation zones may form (stepping stones)

Distribution Phase

  • Large institutions sell shares to the public at high prices
  • Occurs after a prolonged uptrend (markup)
  • Price moves sideways within a trading range
  • Supply gradually overwhelms demand
  • Ends with an "Upthrust" or failed test of resistance

Markdown Phase

  • Price breaks down below the trading range
  • Supply exceeds demand consistently
  • Lower highs and lower lows form a downtrend
  • Panic selling may occur at climax
  • Re-distribution zones may form (stepping stones down)

🎭 The Composite Man

Wyckoff advised traders to imagine all market fluctuations as the result of one operator - the "Composite Man" - who manipulates markets for his benefit. Understanding his strategies allows traders to align their positions with smart money rather than against it. The Composite Man carefully plans, executes, and concludes campaigns to accumulate or distribute shares.

📦 Accumulation Schematic

Accumulation occurs when large operators buy shares at low prices before marking up. The schematic consists of five phases (A-E) with specific events that signal the progression of accumulation.

Key Events in Accumulation

PS
Preliminary Support
Substantial buying begins after a prolonged down-move. Volume increases, signaling the down-move may be ending.
SC
Selling Climax
Panicky selling by the public is absorbed by large interests. Wide spread and heavy volume mark a potential bottom.
AR
Automatic Rally
After intense selling pressure diminishes, buying and short-covering push prices up. Sets the upper boundary of the trading range.
ST
Secondary Test
Price revisits the SC area to test supply/demand balance. Decreased volume and narrower spread confirm the bottom.
Spring
Spring / Shakeout
Price drops below support to trigger stop-losses and trap bears, then quickly reverses back into the range. A bear trap.
Test
Test
After a spring, a successful test makes a higher low on lower volume, indicating supply has been exhausted.
SOS
Sign of Strength
Price advances on increasing spread and volume, confirming demand is now dominant. Often follows a spring.
LPS
Last Point of Support
A pullback on diminished spread and volume after a SOS. Ideal entry point for long positions before markup begins.

📤 Distribution Schematic

Distribution occurs when large operators sell shares to the public at high prices before marking down. It mirrors accumulation but with opposite intent - transferring shares from strong hands to weak hands.

Key Events in Distribution

PSY
Preliminary Supply
Large interests begin selling after a prolonged up-move. Volume expands and spread widens, signaling potential trend change.
BC
Buying Climax
Heavy public buying is met by institutional selling. Often coincides with good news. Marks potential top.
AR
Automatic Reaction
After buying diminishes, heavy supply causes prices to fall. Sets the lower boundary of the distribution range.
ST
Secondary Test
Price revisits the BC area. Decreased volume and narrower spread confirm resistance and supply dominance.
UTAD
Upthrust After Distribution
Price breaks above resistance to trap bulls, then quickly reverses. A bull trap that signals the end of distribution.
SOW
Sign of Weakness
Price drops to or below the trading range support on increased spread and volume. Confirms supply dominance.
LPSY
Last Point of Supply
A weak rally on narrow spread after SOW. Ideal entry point for short positions before markdown begins.

⚖️ Wyckoff's Three Laws

These fundamental laws form the foundation of Wyckoff analysis. They explain how and why markets move, and help traders anticipate future price direction.

1

Supply and Demand

When demand exceeds supply, prices rise. When supply exceeds demand, prices fall. By analyzing price and volume together, traders can assess the balance between supply and demand.

2

Cause and Effect

The horizontal count within a trading range (Cause) determines the potential price movement (Effect). A larger accumulation base suggests a larger subsequent markup.

3

Effort vs Result

Volume (Effort) should confirm price movement (Result). Divergences between effort and result warn of potential trend changes. High volume with little price progress suggests resistance.

✅ Nine Buying/Selling Tests

These tests help determine when a trading range is ending and a new trend is beginning. They define the line of least resistance in the market.

Nine Buying Tests (Accumulation)

1
Downside price objective accomplished
2
Preliminary Support, Selling Climax, Secondary Test present
3
Activity bullish (volume up on rallies, down on reactions)
4
Downward stride/trend line broken
5
Higher lows forming
6
Higher highs forming
7
Stock stronger than the market
8
Base forming (horizontal price line)
9
Reward/risk ratio at least 3:1

Nine Selling Tests (Distribution)

1
Upside objective accomplished
2
Activity bearish (volume down on rallies, up on reactions)
3
Preliminary Supply, Buying Climax present
4
Stock weaker than the market
5
Upward stride/trend line broken
6
Lower highs forming
7
Lower lows forming
8
Crown forming (lateral movement)
9
Reward/risk ratio at least 3:1
